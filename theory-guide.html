First, the building blocks

Western music uses 12 notes that repeat over and over up the keyboard. Seven of them have letter names — C D E F G A B — and then it starts again at C. The black keys are the five in-between notes (sharps ♯ / flats ♭).

Two words you need:

  • Interval — the distance between two notes, counted in half-steps (one piano key to the very next key, black or white). C → C♯ is 1 half-step; C → D is 2.
  • Scale — a chosen ladder of notes inside a key. The C major scale is just the white keys: C D E F G A B. Every chord in this guide is built by stacking notes from a scale.
Why "major"? A major scale always follows the same step pattern (whole-whole-half-whole-whole-whole-half). That fixed pattern is what gives every major key the same bright, "do-re-mi" character — only the starting note changes. In the app, picking a key (C, G, D…) just shifts that same pattern to a new starting note.

Triads — the 3-note chord

A triad is the most basic chord: three notes stacked in thirds. "Stacked in thirds" means you start on a note, skip one scale note, take the next, skip one, take the next:

root  → skip →  third  → skip →  fifth

The three notes are named by how far they sit above the bottom note:

  • Root — the note the chord is named after. A "C chord" has C as its root.
  • Third — two scale-steps up. This note decides whether the chord sounds happy (major) or sad (minor).
  • Fifth — two more scale-steps up. Adds fullness and stability.

In the Harmony drill: your left hand plays the root (low, in the bass clef) and your right hand plays the whole triad (root + third + fifth, in the treble clef). That's why a triad round wants 4 keys total — the root shows up in both hands at different octaves.

The four chord qualities

Keep the root and fifth roughly the same but slide the inner notes a half-step, and the chord changes quality — its emotional color. Four exist; the drill uses the first three (they're the ones that occur naturally in a major key).

QualitySoundsRecipe (half-steps from root)Example
major bright, resolved root, +4 (third), +7 (fifth) C E G
minor dark, soft root, +3 (third), +7 (fifth) C E♭ G
diminished tense, unstable root, +3, +6 (lowered fifth) C E♭ G♭
augmented dreamy, suspended (not in the drill) root, +4, +8 (raised fifth) C E G♯

The only difference between major and minor is the third moving by a single half-step. That one note is the single biggest "happy vs. sad" switch in all of Western music — worth training your ear and eye to spot.

The chords that live in a key

Here's the elegant part. If you build a triad starting on each note of a major scale — using only notes from that scale — you get seven chords, and their qualities always fall in the same pattern. Music labels them with Roman numerals: uppercase = major, lowercase = minor, ° = diminished.

Why this matters more than memorizing 100 chords: Roman numerals are relative to the key. V is "the bright chord a fifth above home" in every key — it's G in C major, D in G major, A in D major. Learn the seven roles once and you can play them in all 12 keys. That's exactly why the drill shows you the numeral next to the chord name.

Progressions — chords in motion

A progression is a short, ordered sequence of these chords. Music almost never sits on one chord; it moves through a few that create a sense of leaving home and coming back. The four in the drill are the most common patterns in all of pop, classical, and jazz — learn them and you can play thousands of songs.

I – IV – V – I  the foundation

Home → away → tension → home. The most fundamental cadence in Western music. In C: C → F → G → C. The V (G) pulls hard back to I (C) — that pull is the engine of most music.

ii – V – I  the jazz turnaround

The single most important progression in jazz, and everywhere in classical too. ii sets up V, which resolves to I. In C: Dm → G → C. Smooth, inevitable-sounding.

I – V – vi – IV  the "4-chord song"

The pop progression — hundreds of hits use exactly this. In C: C → G → Am → F. The vi (a minor chord) adds a touch of bittersweetness without leaving the key.

I – vi – IV – V  the '50s doo-wop

Think Stand By Me. In C: C → Am → F → G. Ends on V, so it loops back to I naturally and can repeat forever.

In the app, "Mixed" picks one of these at random each round, and finishing a progression (in Circle mode) advances you to the next key around the circle of fifths — so over time you see every chord role in every key.

That extra note adds color and tension. The seventh is a "leaning" note — it sounds like it wants to resolve, which makes seventh chords feel richer and more grown-up than plain triads. Jazz is built almost entirely on them.

Putting it together while you practice

When a chord appears in the Harmony drill, read it in this order — it's the same way a fluent musician decodes the page:

  1. Find the root (the bottom note / what your left hand plays).
  2. Check the third — major (bright) or minor (dark)?
  3. Stack the fifth on top for the full triad.
  4. Is there a fourth note? If so it's a seventh chord — add the seventh.
  5. Name the role: glance at the Roman numeral to feel where in the key you are.

Start in Triads, in one key (C is easiest — all white keys). Once the seven chords feel automatic, switch to Mixed to fold in sevenths, then add more keys. The goal isn't memorizing chords — it's getting so fluent at the recipe that you can build any chord in any key on sight.
